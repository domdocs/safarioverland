# Brand voice — Safari Overland

A working reference for anyone writing for the site. Read it once, keep
it open while you work, come back to it when something feels off.

---

## In one sentence

We sound like a discerning friend writing from Victoria Falls,
recommending only the lodges and operators that have left a mark on us
— and saying, plainly, why.

---

## Voice principles

### 1. Restrained luxury

The reference points are Singita, Aman, &Beyond, Wilderness — not
TripAdvisor, not blog-style "10 best safaris" travel content. Their
copy works because it withholds. It trusts the photograph, the rhythm,
the specific noun. We do the same. If a sentence could appear on a
midmarket booking site, it isn't ours yet.

### 2. Sensory but specific

We are allowed sensory language — *the kind of quiet you want, the
smoke that thunders, the slow business of being properly somewhere* —
but only when anchored to something real. Generic poetry ("awaken your
senses," "the journey of a lifetime") is the failure mode. Specific
sensory detail (a track in soft sand, sundowners with the trunk of a
dying acacia for a table, the four-hour drive from camp to airstrip
that *is* the experience) is what we want.

### 3. Editorial, not transactional

We are an opinionated editor's column, not a booking engine. Even on
listings pages — where the practical detail lives — we frame before we
list. *Walking safaris that let you move at nature's pace, creating
space for reflection* before the bullet list of activities. The
practical content stays; we layer the meaning on top.

### 4. The wild is the agent, not the backdrop

This is the hardest discipline and the most important. The reader is
not "experiencing Africa." Africa is doing something to them. Phrases
that follow this rule:

- *what the wild does to you*
- *the slow business of being properly somewhere*
- *the kind that linger long after the dust settles*
- *places that ask something of you*

Phrases that break it (and we don't use):

- *experience the magnificent African bush*
- *immerse yourself in the wonder of nature*
- *embark on a journey of discovery*

### 5. The honest second opinion

We say things other safari sites don't, because we have nothing to
sell. *Crowded crossings. Shoulder-season risk. Hidden costs. Some of
the names everyone uses aren't worth what they ask.* That candour is a
brand asset. Don't smooth it.

## Tone modulation by context

The voice is consistent; the dial moves.

| Context | Dial | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero copy** | Most poetic, fewest words | Earn every line. Three sentences max. *Africa, slowly.* is the tone target. |
| **Section eyebrows** | Mono caps, sparse | E.g. `BY HAND — OPEN` · `THIS WEEK ON THE COLLECTION`. Two to four words. |
| **Body prose** | Warm, specific, occasionally direct | Where most of the work happens. Keep sentences varied — short fragments work, when earned. |
| **Practical info** (FAQ, planning, packing lists) | Clear, helpful, no decoration | The transformational layer is set in the hero; the practical content is just useful. Don't over-decorate cleat-by-cleat info. |
| **Admin / system messages** | Neutral, direct | "Brief saved. Continue when you're ready." Not "Your beautiful brief is safely cradled in the cloud." |
| **Operator outreach / emails** | More personal, slightly warmer | Use first names. Sign with a real person. |
| **Field Notes long-form** | Most flexibility | Can drop into wry, journalistic, even argumentative modes. The brief is "what we wish we'd read before our first trip." Opinionated is the asset. |

## How to evoke transformation without saying "transformative"

This is the trickiest move. Comparator brands almost never say
*transformation* directly. They describe what is happening, and the
reader names it.

**Don't write:**

> A transformative experience that will change your perspective forever.

**Do write:**

> Walking days, slow drives, time enough to read a track. The kind of
> trip that asks something of you.

Three rules:

1. **Show the thing happening to the reader, not the label.** Specific
   acts (walking days, slow drives, reading a track), specific
   sensations (silence, the slow business of being properly
   somewhere). Let the label arrive on its own.
2. **Trust the silence.** Don't summarise the feeling for the reader.
   *"The kind that linger long after the dust settles"* trusts them to
   feel what *linger* means.
3. **Avoid the words.** *Transformative, transformational, transform,
   life-changing, journey of discovery, awaken*. They signal that we
   know we should be doing this and have substituted the label for the
   thing. Forbidden in user-facing copy.

## Lexicon — phrases we reuse

These are the brand's signature lines. Use them; vary them; rotate
them. They're chosen for restraint, sensory weight, and earned
emotion.

1. *what the wild does to you, not just what it shows you*
2. *trips that linger long after the dust settles*
3. *the kind of quiet you want*
4. *a closer look, a slower pace, a moment to listen*
5. *places that ask something of you*
6. *what these places give back*
7. *a smaller circle of operators*
8. *we list the lodges we'd send our friends to*
9. *the slow business of being properly somewhere*
10. *travel that leaves a mark*

### Brand-specific vocabulary

- **The collection** — never *the directory*. We are no longer one.
- **By hand** — the trip-planning service. (Internal code still uses
  *briefs* for the table; user-facing language is "by hand" or "send
  us a brief".)
- **Field Notes** — long-form editorial content. Capital F, capital
  N. Never *blog*, never *articles*.
- **The atlas** — `/destinations`. The regional anchor pages.
- **Three things** — our manifesto structure. Always three.
- **From Victoria Falls** — our geographic anchor. Use it. We are not
  a faceless directory; we are based somewhere specific. *Mosi-oa-Tunya*
  is allowed and welcomed.
- **Operators / lodges / camps** — the things we list. Avoid *property*
  (real-estate-y), *accommodation* (sterile), *resort* (almost always
  wrong for our register).
- **Travellers** — the people we serve. Avoid *guests* (that's the
  operator's word), *clients* (transactional), *customers* (no).

### Format conventions

- **Eyebrow**: mono caps, ≤4 words. Used to cue the section.
- **Headline**: serif, italic-amber accent on one word that carries
  the meaning. *Africa, slowly.* / *A safari, drawn **by hand**.* /
  *Three things we promise to do **properly**.*
- **Subhead**: italic serif, longer, evocative.
- **Body**: sans, restrained. Em dashes welcome. Numbered lists in
  mono caps (`01` / `02` / `03`).
- **Single-word italic accents**: gold (`text-amber`), one or two per
  page maximum. Overused, they become a tic.

## The twelve unforgivables

Words and constructions that should never appear in user-facing copy.

1. **transformative**, **transformational**, **transform** — we let the reader name the experience
2. **unforgettable**, **once-in-a-lifetime**, **life-changing** — empty boasts
3. **amazing**, **incredible**, **breathtaking**, **stunning** — generic intensifiers
4. **embark**, **odyssey**, **journey of discovery** — purple
5. **awaken your senses** — Singita-flavoured, becomes a cliché if we say it
6. **discover** — used as a transitive verb in marketing copy ("discover Africa") it's hollow; reserve for genuine exploration ("we discovered the lodge had changed hands")
7. **passionate** — never "we are passionate about safaris"
8. **luxury** as an adjective applied to Safari Overland (we don't claim it; the operators we list might be luxurious, we are an editor)
9. **state-of-the-art**, **best-in-class**, **world-class** — corporate
10. **!** — exclamation marks. Anywhere. Even in headers. The only acceptable place is inside dialogue quotes within Field Notes
11. **Title-Case headlines** — we use sentence case (or italic accent for emphasis). *Africa, slowly.* not *Africa, Slowly.*
12. **Every** as a sweeping marketing adjective — "every traveller deserves," "on every safari," etc. Specific is stronger.

## Annotated rewrites — examples from the work we shipped

### Hero subhead

**Before** (generic directory framing):
> Curated lodges, operators, and field notes from across the continent. The kind of trips we'd want to take ourselves.

**After** (curated collection, transformational layer):
> A small collection of lodges and operators chosen for what the wild does to you — not just what it shows you.

What changed: the framing shifted from "what we offer" (lodges, operators, notes) to "what they do to you." The list of nouns is gone; one specific image is doing all the work.

### Three-pillar manifesto

**Before** (operational promises):
> 01 Curated, not crowdsourced.
> 02 Honest about tradeoffs.
> 03 On the ground, year-round.

**After** (one operational, one transformational, one regenerative):
> 01 Curation, not catalogue.
> 02 Trips that leave a mark.
> 03 Travel with consequence.

What changed: 02 was the lever. *Trips that leave a mark* puts the reader at the centre. 03 was reframed from "we are present" to "your booking funds the work" — the reader is part of something bigger.

### Mission statement (About)

**Before**:
> Connect travellers with safaris that stand up to scrutiny.

**After**:
> Send travellers to safaris that change them.

What changed: *stand up to scrutiny* is editor-language (defensive, looking outward). *Safaris that change them* is reader-language (the thing happening, to them). Stronger because it's about the outcome, not the credential.

### Trip-builder rename

**Before**:
> TRIP BUILDER — OPEN
> Tell us what you want from a safari. We'll send back three options in 48 hours.

**After**:
> BY HAND — OPEN
> A safari, drawn by hand.
> Tell us what you're chasing — the months, the rhythm, the kind of quiet you want.

What changed: *trip builder* is product-management vocabulary; *by hand* is craft vocabulary. *What you want from a safari* is transactional; *what you're chasing* is personal. The added line about *the kind of quiet you want* is the transformational layer — we name the goal as a sensation, not an itinerary.

## House style

- **British English**: *favour, travellers, organisation, programme, neighbour, behaviour, colour*. The *Mosi-oa-Tunya / The Smoke That Thunders* register sets the cultural tone; British-English spelling matches it. (Caveat: brand and product names retain their own spellings — Trip Builder is a name, but we don't write "trip builder" any more.)
- **Em dashes** are welcome — they sit naturally inside the cadence and pair well with serif typography. Single em dash, no spaces, *or* spaces around it consistently within a piece.
- **Sentence rhythm** is varied. Short sentence. A longer one that lets a clause breathe and another beat land. A fragment.
- **Numbered lists**: `01` / `02` / `03` in mono caps. Always two digits up to nine.
- **Numbers** in body prose: spell out one to nine, numerals from 10. *Three things we promise to do properly* not *3 things*.
- **Currency / prices**: only when meaningful. Most of the time, *budget* or *price point* is enough. When we do show, we show the operator's own framing (*From $450 per person per night, all-inclusive*).
- **Quotation marks**: curly ("smart") quotes when typeset. The brand mark itself uses inverted-comma typographic quotes — we don't try to recreate those in body text.
- **Capitalisation**: *Field Notes* as a noun phrase (capital). *the collection* lowercase. *Victoria Falls* always capitalised. *Mosi-oa-Tunya* as proper noun, with em dash if defined inline (*Mosi-oa-Tunya — the smoke that thunders*).

## When in doubt

Ask: *would a magazine editor of an old, careful travel quarterly write this?*

If yes, ship it. If no, cut a sentence and try again.
