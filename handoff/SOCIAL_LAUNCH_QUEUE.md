> The directory is gone.
>
> When we built safarioverland.com — different industry, different
> time — the idea was a clearing house. Lodges, operators, agents,
> every kind of safari, in one place. It was useful. It was also
> generic. The kind of site you'd recommend without quite
> recommending.
>
> We've been quietly rewriting it for months.
>
> What's gone live this week is smaller, slower, and more honest. A
> small collection of lodges and operators we'd send our friends to.
> Each one chosen for what the wild does to you, not just what it
> shows you. Walking-safari camps where the guide can read a track.
> Conservancies where your bed-night levy keeps a ranger employed.
> Owner-run camps with founders who'll still be at dinner.
>
> We've cut more than we've kept, and we're going to keep cutting.
>
> We've also started writing properly — Field Notes, on the things we
> wish we'd read before our first trip. East versus southern. Luxury
> versus budget. Why community conservancies are the most quietly
> important thing happening in African travel.
>
> The other shift: how we plan trips.
>
> If you're considering a safari this year, send us a brief — the
> months you can travel, the regions you've been thinking about, the
> kind of quiet you want. We'll come back with three drawn-by-hand
> routes inside 48 hours. No commission. No markup. Just an honest
> reading of where you'll be best placed, and an introduction to the
> people on the ground.
>
> It's a strange time to launch a smaller version of something. The
> pressure on travel sites is to be larger, faster, more
> comprehensive. We're going the other way on purpose.
>
> From Victoria Falls,
>
> Niels

> A short note on subtraction.
>
> When we audited the site last month, we cut more listings than we
> kept. Some of the cuts were obvious — operators we'd never visited,
> agents whose business was reselling other agents. Some weren't —
> properties we'd been to, properties with strong reviews, properties
> we liked.
>
> The bar we set, in the end, wasn't quality. It was specificity. Did
> this place earn a sentence? Did it offer something only it could? Or
> was it just good?
>
> A lot of safari operators are good. The ones we kept did one more
> thing. They asked something of you.
>
> The bar will keep moving. Some properties on the list this morning
> won't be there next year. New ones will arrive. The work of an
> editor, more than a directory.
>

> How we plan a safari, in three steps.
>
> Step 1 — the brief. A page, sometimes two. Not a form so much as a
> conversation. The months you can travel. The regions you've been
> thinking about. The rhythm — fast, slow, mixed. The kind of quiet
> you want. Whether you need walking and whether you'd rather camp-hop.
> Children, if any. Mobility, if relevant.
>
> Step 2 — the routes. We come back with three. At three different
> rhythms, three different prices. Each one is a real itinerary, with
> named operators we've spoken to, season-aware, with the trade-offs
> flagged. *September is great for game in the Mara but you'll be
> sharing crossings. June is better for solitude but the migration's
> south of you.* The honest version, not the brochure version.
>
> Step 3 — the introduction. You pick a route. We introduce you to the
> operators directly. They handle the booking. We don't take
> commission, we don't add markup, we don't sit between you and the
> people running the camps.
>
> The whole thing takes 48 hours, end to end. We turn down briefs we
> can't do well, and we'll tell you when a different specialist is a
> better fit than we are.
>
> If you've got a trip in your head already — months, regions, rhythm
> — the brief form's open on the site.
>
> Niels

### Post 11

> The most quietly important thing in African travel.
>
> Most people who come on safari think the conservation work is
> happening in national parks. National parks matter. But the model
> that's actually scaling — in places like northern Kenya, the Greater
> Mara, the Okavango buffer, parts of Namibia — is community
> conservancies.
>
> Here's the gist. A community owns its ancestral land. It enters into
> a lease with a small number of safari operators. Travellers stay;
> the operators pay the community a per-night levy and a lease; the
> community keeps the land wild because that's what generates income.
> The rangers are local. The school is funded. The borehole is dug.
> The wildlife corridor stays open because the people on either side
> of it have a reason to keep it open.
>
> It's slower and messier than the national-park model. It depends on
> a working partnership between a community trust, a small number of
> operators, and an external NGO or two. When it works, it works well.
> When it doesn't, the lease lapses and the land gets fenced or farmed.
>
> The operators we've kept on the new safarioverland.com almost all
> sit inside this model. Not because we were trying to be virtuous —
> because the camps inside conservancies tend to be the better camps.
> Smaller, more specific, more guided by people who actually live
> there. They make for better trips.
>
> If you're researching a safari this year, ask the operator: *what's
> the conservancy structure here? what's the community share? what's
> the lease length?* If the answer is a marketing paragraph, find a
> different operator.
>
> Niels

## After week 4 — the ongoing rhythm

Once this series is done, the cadence stays the same (Mon IG, Tue
LinkedIn, Fri IG) but drops the "launch arc" structure. Mix freely
from these recurring formats:

- **In-season this week** — what's worth seeing where, right now.
  Drives planning-service inbound.
- **A kept-list reveal** — one specific lodge per month, with
  permissioned photography and the founder note.
- **A Field Note teaser** — when an article lands, post the pull-quote.
- **A practical Field Note** — when a planning guide lands, post the
  three-line summary.
- **A founder POV** — Niels weighing in on something current
  (a season, a development, an operator's news).
- **A conservation moment** — a milestone, a result, a story behind
  one of the conservancies on the list.

The mix should land roughly: 2 in-season + 1 kept-list + 1 Field Note
+ 1 POV + 1 conservation per month, give or take.
