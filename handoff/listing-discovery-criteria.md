# Listing discovery — criteria framework

The first step of the listing pipeline. Before we write up any property,
we surface candidates for a region. This document defines what we're
looking for and how to detect it from public information.

Use this with any region: feed the criteria + the region name into a
Cowork session, get back a tier-ranked shortlist.

---

## What we're looking for

The brand-fit qualities, mapped to public signals you can verify from
the operator's website, review aggregators, and industry sources.

### Scale — small

A property we'd recommend has under ~20 rooms. Above that, the camp
loses the intimacy and the editorial register we write in. Exceptions
exist (multi-camp operators with small individual camps) but the unit
of measurement is the camp, not the company.

**Public signal:** the operator's "About" or "Rooms" page lists the
capacity. We want six to twenty.

### Operator — founder-led, owner-run, or small-group hand

The kind of property where the founder will still be at dinner.
Exceptions for the gold-standard small groups (Singita, &Beyond,
Wilderness, Great Plains, Time + Tide) when the individual camp is
demonstrably hand-built.

**Public signal:** the "About us" page tells the story. If the page
reads as corporate hotel copy or you can't find a founder named
anywhere, that's a flag.

### Pace — slow

Walking safaris, multi-night minimums, mokoro days, sundowner rituals,
sleep-outs. Properties that ask something of the traveller rather
than ticking the Big Five in three days.

**Public signal:** the activities list emphasises walking, water, and
unhurried days. Tick-list game-drive language is a flag. Two-night
minimum or more is a positive signal.

### Conservation — specific and named

A documented conservation partnership, a bed-night levy paid into a
community trust, a conservancy lease, or a relationship with a named
anti-poaching unit.

**Public signal:** the conservation page exists and names partners.
*"We are passionate about wildlife"* without a named programme is a
flag. *"$80 per bed-night to Painted Dog Conservation"* is exactly
what we want.

### Community — specific and ongoing

Documented community work — school funding, local hiring percentages,
projects with named beneficiaries.

**Public signal:** same shape as conservation. Names of schools or
boreholes funded. Local-employment numbers. Multi-year commitment
language. Vague *"giving back"* phrasing is a flag.

### Design — intentional

Architecture and interiors that read as considered, not generic safari
kitsch. Natural materials, restrained palette, regional craft.

**Public signal:** the photography itself does the work. Look at the
operator's hero images. If they could pass on the cover of *Condé Nast
Traveller* or in the *Singita* portfolio, the design bar is being met.
If they look like generic stock safari shots, that's a flag.

### Reviews — positive and consistent

A property reviewed at 4.7+ on TripAdvisor and Google over 100+
reviews, with the negative reviews citing weather or wildlife
unpredictability rather than service or operator failures.

**Public signal:** aggregate rating, count of reviews, what the
critical reviews actually say. Repeat-guest mentions are gold.

### Wellness / extras — bonus

Yoga, spa, silent walks, sleep-outs, art residencies, photography
guides. None required; presence indicates editorial depth.

## What we screen out

Red flags that knock a property off the shortlist regardless of
other strengths:

- Mass-market positioning, *"fun for the whole family"* language
- Inclusive-holiday or all-resort branding (Sandals-shaped properties)
- Heavy Big Five tick-listing without conservation context
- Generic marketing copy without specific substance
- *Luxury* used as a brand adjective without earning it
- Day-tripper-primary destinations
- No conservation or community programme mentioned anywhere
- Owned by a hotel chain (Marriott, Hilton, Accor) — exceptions only
  if the individual property has retained its independence editorially
- Aggressively booked through OTAs (Expedia, Booking.com primary)
  with no direct-booking option
- Aggressive use of *transformative / unforgettable / journey of
  discovery* — they're using the words we won't, which usually means
  the substance isn't there either

## How to apply

For any region, run this query in a Cowork session:

> Using `handoff/listing-discovery-criteria.md`, propose a shortlist of
> 8–12 properties in [REGION] that fit the criteria. Rank into Tier A
> (likely fit), Tier B (worth investigating), Tier C (probably wrong
> but worth knowing about). For each, give: name, location, ownership,
> capacity, one-line brand-fit rationale, and what's uncertain.

The output is a shortlist Dom can confirm or reject in 15 minutes,
based on his actual knowledge of the area and the people running things.

After confirmation, each kept property moves into the per-listing
pipeline (research → AI draft → verify → operator outreach → publish).

---

## Important: data freshness

This whole approach is built on training-data knowledge plus public
sources. The African safari industry shifts:

- Properties change hands (Singita acquired by Singita; Wilderness
  Safaris through the Vivili era; Time + Tide's recent acquisitions)
- Founders retire or sell
- Camps close, new camps open
- Conservation partnerships start and end

Any AI-generated shortlist needs Dom's local-knowledge filter as a
mandatory step. Don't publish anything from a shortlist without that
filter. Treat the shortlist as a research starting point, not a
ready-to-publish output.
